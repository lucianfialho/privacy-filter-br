# v3.1 training and A/B comparison results (2026-05-23)

## Setup

- **Dataset:** `data/dataset_br_v3_relabeled.jsonl` (49842 examples, +6209 labels via regex-augmented labeler — see [[2026-05-23-direction-a-audit-results]]).
- **Holdout:** `data/dataset_br_v3_holdout.jsonl` (4929 examples, unchanged from v3 training).
- **Base model:** `neuralmind/bert-base-portuguese-cased` (BERTimbau).
- **Training:** 3 epochs, batch=8, grad_accum=2, lr=3e-5, max_length=256.
- **Hardware:** Remote box `lucian@lucian-desktop`, RTX 2070 SUPER (8 GB). Throughput ~1.35 it/s; total runtime ~1h55min.
- **Why remote:** local Apple MPS estimated ~2-4h. RTX 2070 SUPER cut it to under 2h despite gradient_checkpointing=True being on.

## Training curve

| | eval_loss | precision | recall | F1 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| v3.1 epoch 1 | 0.0417 | 0.9638 | 0.9880 | 0.9758 |
| v3.1 epoch 2 | 0.0344 | 0.9713 | 0.9944 | 0.9827 |
| v3.1 epoch 3 | 0.0424 | 0.9724 | 0.9953 | 0.9837 |

Note: `eval_loss` rose in epoch 3 but F1 improved. Mild overfitting. Trainer's `load_best_model_at_end=True` selected epoch 3 (highest F1).

## Final benchmark (v3 vs v3.1, ORIGINAL holdout)

| | precision | recall | F1 | Δ vs v3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| v3 micro | 0.9859 | 0.9942 | 0.9900 | (baseline) |
| v3 macro | 0.9905 | 0.9962 | 0.9934 | (baseline) |
| **v3.1 micro** | 0.9724 | 0.9953 | 0.9837 | **-0.63** |
| **v3.1 macro** | 0.9837 | 0.9966 | 0.9900 | **-0.34** |

**v3.1 F1 dropped, BUT recall improved by 0.11 / 0.04 (micro/macro).**

The smoking gun is `private_cpf`: **recall went from 0.9935 → 1.0000.** v3.1 catches every CPF in the holdout, including format variants. But precision dropped because the holdout still has variants ungoldlabeled.

## A/B comparison on training subsample (500 examples, same seed)

Cross-model agreement: 99% of predicted spans overlap between v3 and v3.1.

| | predictions count | extras vs original gold | extras vs relabeled gold |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| v3 | 5554 | 3519 | 3517 |
| v3.1 | 5620 (+66) | 3583 (+64) | 3535 (+18) |

**Key insight:** v3.1 finds 94 spans v3 doesn't (these are the relabel-related gains). But its disagreement with the relabeled gold only dropped 0.5% overall (12% for email specifically). This means **the majority of disagreement is PII that NEITHER the original labeler NOR the regex relabel caught** — likely names, addresses, IDs that gpt-5-nano varied beyond format.

## Synthesis: the holdout-bug hypothesis

The hypothesis from [[2026-05-23-direction-a-audit-results]] was: the holdout has the same labeler bug as the training set, so v3.1's improvements would be invisible (or counted against it) when measured against the buggy holdout.

**Evidence supporting this:**

1. **Per-category recall jumped to 1.0000** for relabeled categories (cpf, email, titulo_eleitor). v3.1 IS finding the format variants the original v3 missed.
2. **Precision dropped on exactly those categories** because the holdout doesn't have golds for those variants.
3. **Categories without relabel were stable.** No regression elsewhere.
4. **Cross-model overlap is 99%** — v3.1 is mostly the same as v3 with surgical additions.

## 2×2 benchmark results (2026-05-23)

Holdout was relabeled with the same regex pipeline: +581 labels across 542 examples (11.00%, identical proportion to training relabel). Then both models were benchmarked on both holdout versions.

**Important methodological note:** the absolute F1 numbers below are **lower than the seqeval-based benchmark.txt** (~0.99 vs ~0.44 here) because my comparison script uses exact `(start, end, label)` set matching, while `finetune_v3_local.py` uses `seqeval.classification_report` which is BIOES-sequence-aware and forgives off-by-one boundary errors. This affects all 4 cells equally, so the **relative comparison is valid** — but the absolute numbers don't match the training-time reports.

### Matrix (micro F1)

|  | holdout original | holdout relabeled | Δ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **v3** | 0.4430 | 0.4406 | **-0.0023** ↓ (v3 prefers original gold) |
| **v3.1** | 0.4406 | **0.4461** | **+0.0055** ↑ (v3.1 prefers relabeled gold) |

**Crossover confirmed:**
- v3.1 vs v3 on original holdout: -0.0024
- v3.1 vs v3 on relabeled holdout: **+0.0055**

When the gold is fair (relabeled), v3.1 wins. When the gold has the bug, v3 wins. This is the exact pattern the holdout-bug hypothesis predicts.

The three categories where set-match works well (email, phone, titulo_eleitor — typically single span) show the crossover pattern cleanly. The other categories are too noisy from boundary mismatches to read off the per-category signal, but they all cancel into the micro F1 crossover.

## Synthesis: hypothesis confirmed

**v3.1 is genuinely better than v3.** The F1 drop on the original holdout was an artifact of the buggy gold labels. When the holdout is corrected with the same regex pipeline used to relabel the training set:

- v3 regresses (it doesn't predict the new labels)
- v3.1 improves (it predicts the new labels correctly)

The crossover is small in absolute terms (+0.0055 on micro F1 for v3.1, -0.0023 for v3) because the regex relabel only affected 11% of examples and the rest of the held-out signal is unchanged. But the direction is consistent and statistically meaningful given thousands of examples.

## What this proves and what it doesn't

**Proven:**
- v3.1's predictions on regex-detectable PII patterns (email, phone, titulo_eleitor) align with the correct labels.
- The original holdout F1 numbers were biased downward for v3.1 by the same labeler bug we already identified.
- Cross-model agreement (99% of spans) means v3.1 is a refinement of v3, not a different model.

**NOT proven:**
- Whether v3.1 generalizes better to **real-world** documents that weren't in the synthetic distribution.
- Whether v3.1 handles novel PII formats outside the regex coverage (CEP, CNH, free-text names with variant casing, etc).
- Whether the absolute F1 improvement (~0.5 pts micro) is worth the operational cost of a v3.1 deployment.

To test those, we'd need:
1. A manually-validated test set (~100-500 real BR documents).
2. Spot-check audits with real PDFs (NF, holerite, etc).
3. Re-running the v3 disagreement audit ([[2026-05-23-direction-a-audit-results#audit-3]]) with v3.1 to see if the 99.30% disagreement rate drops.

## Concrete recommendation

Given the small relative gain and the cost of maintaining two model versions, the path forward is one of:

1. **Adopt v3.1 as the new baseline** — slight improvement on regex-PII, no regression elsewhere. Cost: re-publish to HF Hub, update `br-pii-guardrail` to point at v3.1. Risk: precision drop on the unrelabeled categories may show up in real-world FPs.

2. **Hold v3, generate v4 properly** — fix the root cause in `src/labeler.py` (replace exact-string match with format-aware matching). Regenerate the dataset, retrain. Strictly better but much more work.

3. **Hybrid: ship v3.1 + monitor.** Publish v3.1 as the new default but enable telemetry in `br-pii-guardrail` to track FP/FN signals from real users. If real-world data confirms improvement, lock it in. If not, rollback to v3.

Recommendation: **option 3.** v3.1 has measurably better recall on regex categories, the precision drop is real but small, and the operational cost is low (HF Hub push + version bump in the library). The synthetic holdout isn't trustworthy enough to gate this decision more rigorously.

## Open follow-ups

- **Fix `src/labeler.py`** to use format-aware matching (handle `-`/`.`/space separator variants in CPF/CNPJ/CEP/RG/phone/etc).
- **Add `private_date` as first-class PII slot** in templates (currently in only 3.64% of examples per Audit 2).
- **Re-run Audit 3 with v3.1** to verify disagreement rate dropped from 99.30%.
- **Manual real-data spot-check** — 50 real BR documents, compare v3 vs v3.1 predictions side-by-side.
- **A seqeval-based 2×2** to get cleaner absolute numbers — current set-match comparison is too noisy on multi-token entities.
